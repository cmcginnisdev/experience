Agile Manifesto

Main Point: Agile Manifesto prioritizes individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, and responding to change over following a plan.

Details: Codified in 2001 in Snowbird by Scrum, XP, and DSDM practitioners. These values are fundamental to why agile works and continues to be used on projects with high uncertainty today.

Sprint Basics

Main Point: Sprint basics include Sprint Planning, Sprint Development, and Sprint Retro & Review, with a Sprint being a timebox for completing work, usually ranging from two weeks to a month.

Details: Sprint Planning starts with the Product Owner selecting work from the Product Backlog. Sprint Development focuses on daily reporting and execution against selected stories. Sprint Retro & Review are ceremonies for gaining feedback and driving continuous improvement.

Sprint Development

Main Point: Sprint Development begins with the daily standup, focusing on daily reporting and execution against selected stories to deliver a shippable product increment.

Details: Teams analyze, build, and test the work, facilitated by the Scrum Master. Meetings are held, and the Product Owner determines if a Story is complete to meet stakeholder needs.

Sprint Retro & Review

Main Point: Sprint Retro and Review are ceremonies to gain feedback and drive continuous improvement into the team.

Details: Sprint Review allows stakeholders to provide feedback on the product increment. Sprint Retrospective evaluates the sprint and identifies opportunities for improvement in the next sprint.

Iron Triangle

Main Point: The Iron Triangle includes Scope, Schedule, and Budget, which are constraints and costs to the organization in project management.

Details: More schedule means a delay of project benefits, more budget means more capital invested, and more scope means a larger product to support or maintain.

Project Management Methods

Main Point: Three types of project management are Agile, Traditional, and Lean, each varying constraints on scope, schedule, and budget.

Details: Agile focuses on speed, Traditional on efficiency, and Lean on innovation. Understanding the goals and requirements of each method is essential for effective project management.

False Comparisons

Main Point: False comparisons across types of projects abound, such as objections that Agile is missing critical elements like design, testing, or documentation.

Details: Every successful project must have key elements including a charter, plan, documentation, design, and testing. Agile methodologies prioritize varying scope to target what the customer needs, delivering a minimum viable product (MVP).

Agile Project Essentials

Main Point: Agile projects require shared vision robust to change, whole teams, incremental delivery, and continuous integration & testing.

Details: Frameworks like Scrum, SAFe, or Disciplined Agile help define roles and processes to scale Agile methodologies, providing a shared language while the method remains consistent.
